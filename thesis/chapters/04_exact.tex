\chapter{Exact inference for the Normalized Power Prior}
\label{sec:exact}

In this section, we develop exact Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods for inference with the normalized power prior (NPP).
The key challenge lies in handling the intractable normalizing constant, which we address through  Russian-roulette estimators that preserve the exactness of our sampling scheme.

\section{Barker's Method via Poisson Estimator}

For the NPP, the joint posterior is:
\begin{equation}
\pi(\theta, \eta | D, D_0) \propto \frac{1}{c_0(\eta)} L(D|\theta) L_0(D_0|\theta)^\eta \pi_0(\theta) \pi_A(\eta)
\end{equation}

The challenging term is the normalizing constant:
\begin{equation}
c_0(\eta) = \int_\Theta L_0(D_0|\theta)^\eta \pi_0(\theta) \dd{\theta}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Updating $\theta$ given $\eta$}
\comEA{Here I suggest substituting to regular MH}

For updating $\theta$, the full conditional is:
\begin{equation}
\pi(\theta | \eta, D, D_0) \propto L(D|\theta) L_0(D_0|\theta)^\eta \pi_0(\theta)
\end{equation}

Since $L_0(D_0|\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{N_0} f_i(\theta | D_{0i})$, we can use DCBF.
For instance, when we have Gaussian data: $f_i(\theta | y_i) = \mathcal{N}(y_i | \theta, \Sigma)$.

The ratio form for Barker's algorithm is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\pi(\theta'|\eta)}{\pi(\theta|\eta)} = \frac{L(D|\theta')}{L(D|\theta)} \cdot \frac{\pi_0(\theta')}{\pi_0(\theta)} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{N_0} \left(\frac{f_i(\theta'|D_{0i})}{f_i(\theta|D_{0i})}\right)^\eta
\end{equation}

The first two ratios are tractable.
For the product term, we construct factor coins using likelihood ratios, which benefit from posterior concentration.

\subsection{Updating $\eta$ given $\theta$}

Updating $\eta$ is more subtle because the full conditional involves $c_0(\eta)$:
\begin{equation}
\pi(\eta | \theta, D, D_0) \propto \frac{\pi_A(\eta)}{c_0(\eta)} L_0(D_0|\theta)^\eta
\end{equation}

The ratio becomes:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\pi(\eta'|\theta)}{\pi(\eta|\theta)} = \frac{\pi_A(\eta')}{\pi_A(\eta)} \cdot \frac{c_0(\eta)}{c_0(\eta')} \cdot L_0(D_0|\theta)^{\eta' - \eta}
\end{equation}

The term $c_0(\eta)/c_0(\eta')$ is intractable but can be estimated using


\begin{equation}
  \dv{\tau}\log c_0(\tau) = \bE_{\theta\sim\pi_\tau(\theta)} \left[\log L_0(D_0\mid\theta)\right],
\end{equation}

where $\pi_\tau(\theta) \propto L_0(D_0\mid\theta)^\tau \pi_0(\theta)$ is the power posterior at temperature $\tau$.

Integrating this identity from $\eta$ to $\eta'$ yields the log-ratio:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta = \log\frac{c_0(\eta')}{c_0(\eta)} = \int_{\eta}^{\eta'} \bE_{\theta\sim\pi_\tau(\theta)} \left[\log L_0(D_0\mid\theta)\right] \, \dd{\tau}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Practical implementation}

In practice, we replace the integral $\Delta$ with a simple Monte Carlo estimation. This is a unbiased estimator for the actual integral:

\begin{align}
  \widehat{\Delta}_a(\eta, \eta') &= \dfrac{(\eta' - \eta)}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \bE_{\theta\sim\pi_{\tau_k}(\theta)} \left[\log L_0(D_0\mid\theta)\right], \text{ and so} \\
  \widehat{\Delta}_b(\eta, \eta') &=  \dfrac{(\eta' - \eta)}{M\cdot K} \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{m=1}^M \log L_0\qty(D_0\mid\widetilde{\theta}_{m_k}),
\end{align}

where $\tau_k \sim \Uni(\eta, \eta')$, and $\qty(\widetilde{\theta}_{m_k} \mid \tau_k) \sim \pi_{\tau_k}(\theta)$.


Thus, for a single sample $\tau_\star \sim \Uni(\eta, \eta')$, and $\qty(\widetilde{\theta}_{\star} \mid \tau_\star) \sim \pi_{\tau_\star}(\theta)$ we have:\comEA{Because it doesn't depend on the variance}

\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\Delta}(\eta, \eta') = (\eta' - \eta) \log L_0\qty(D_0\mid\widetilde{\theta}_{\star})
\end{equation}


\subsection{Producing an unbiased estimator via Poisson Estimator}

As discussed in~\cite{stumpf2025}, we can produce an unbiased estimator $\widehat{I}$ such that $\bE_{K, (\tau_\star, \theta_\star)_1, \dots, (\tau_\star, \theta_\star)_K}\qty[\widehat{I}] = \exp{-\Delta}$, where $K\sim \Poi(\lambda)$ and $(\tau_\star, \theta_\star)_j \sim P$ (built from the conditional and marginal). In fact, we sample ${\tau_\star}_j \sim \Uni[\eta, \eta']$.
\comEA{Note here, we have to define this measure properly. It should be a product measure.}

In that case, we find 

\begin{equation}
  \widehat{I} = \exp\{(\lambda -c)\}\prod_{j=1}^{K}\dfrac{(c - \widehat{\Delta}({\tau_\star}_j))}{\lambda}
  \label{eq:exact-est}
\end{equation}


The choice of $\lambda$ and $c$ is crucial for the efficiency of the estimator. An optimal choice would be to take $\lambda = \sup_{\theta \in \Theta} \widehat{\Delta} - \inf_{\theta \in \Theta} \widehat{\Delta}$ and $c =  \sup_{\theta \in \Theta} \widehat{\Delta}$. Refer to section ~\ref{proof:exact-est} for a detailed proof of unbiasedness.

In order to make proper use of this method with $\widehat{\Delta}(\eta, \eta')$, one would use a prior with finite support.
We then suggest employing a standard-normal truncated at the 99\% percentiles.
